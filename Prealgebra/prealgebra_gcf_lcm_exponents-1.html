Greatest Common Factor & Least Common Multiple

📋
Criterion 01
Description

The Greatest Common Factor (GCF) — also called the Greatest Common Divisor (GCD) — is the largest whole number that divides evenly into two or more numbers without leaving a remainder.

The Least Common Multiple (LCM) is the smallest positive whole number that is a multiple of two or more numbers — meaning all the given numbers divide into it evenly.

These two concepts are inverses of each other in spirit: GCF asks "what's the biggest shared divisor?" while LCM asks "what's the smallest shared multiple?" They are deeply related through the formula:

GCF(a, b) × LCM(a, b) = a × b

Both rely on understanding factors (numbers that divide into a value) and multiples (numbers a value divides into).

🎯
Criterion 02
Purpose
FractionsGCF is used to simplify fractions to their lowest terms — dividing numerator and denominator by their GCF.
FractionsLCM is used to find common denominators when adding or subtracting fractions.
AlgebraGCF is the first step in factoring polynomials — you always factor out the GCF before anything else.
DataIn data analytics, GCF and LCM underlie data normalization, frequency alignment, and interval matching (e.g., aligning time series sampled at different rates).
LogicLCM is used in scheduling problems — finding when two repeating events will coincide again.
⚙️
Criterion 03
How This Works

Method 1 — Listing Factors/Multiples (Simple, small numbers)

GCF of 12 and 18:
Factors of 12 → 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12
Factors of 18 → 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18
Common factors → 1, 2, 3, 6
Greatest → GCF = 6

LCM of 4 and 6:
Multiples of 4 → 4, 8, 12, 16, 20...
Multiples of 6 → 6, 12, 18, 24...
First common → LCM = 12

Method 2 — Prime Factorization (Reliable for any numbers)

1
Break each number into its prime factors using a factor tree or repeated division.
2
For GCF: take only the prime factors that appear in both numbers, using the lower exponent.
3
For LCM: take all prime factors that appear in either number, using the higher exponent.
4
Multiply the selected prime factors together to get your answer.

Method 3 — Euclidean Algorithm (GCF only, fastest for large numbers)

GCF(a, b): divide a by b, replace a with b, b with remainder.
Repeat until remainder = 0. The last non-zero remainder is the GCF.

GCF(48, 18):
48 ÷ 18 = 2 remainder 12 → GCF(18, 12)
18 ÷ 12 = 1 remainder 6 → GCF(12, 6)
12 ÷ 6 = 2 remainder 0 → GCF = 6

Criterion 04
Sample Problems
Sample A — GCF via Prime Factorization
Find GCF(36, 60)

36 = 2² × 3²
60 = 2² × 3 × 5

Common primes: 2² and 3¹ (take lower exponents)
GCF = 2² × 3 = 4 × 3 = 12
✔ Check: 36 ÷ 12 = 3 ✓ and 60 ÷ 12 = 5 ✓ — both divide evenly.
Sample B — LCM via Prime Factorization
Find LCM(8, 14)

8 = 2³
14 = 2 × 7

All primes: 2³ and 7¹ (take higher exponents)
LCM = 2³ × 7 = 8 × 7 = 56
✔ Check: 56 ÷ 8 = 7 ✓ and 56 ÷ 14 = 4 ✓ — both divide evenly.
Sample C — Simplify a Fraction Using GCF
Simplify: 48/72

GCF(48, 72) = ?
48 = 2⁴ × 3
72 = 2³ × 3²
GCF = 2³ × 3 = 24

48 ÷ 24 = 2
72 ÷ 24 = 3
Result: 2/3
✔ 2/3 is fully simplified — GCF(2,3) = 1, no further reduction possible.
Sample D — Real-World LCM (Scheduling)
Event A repeats every 6 days.
Event B repeats every 8 days.
When do they happen on the same day again?

LCM(6, 8):
6 = 2 × 3
8 = 2³
LCM = 2³ × 3 = 24 days
✔ Both events coincide every 24 days from the start.

Criterion 05
Common Challenges & How to Solve Them
Confusing GCF and LCM — using one where the other is needed.
Fix: Remember the purpose. Simplifying a fraction? → GCF (make smaller). Finding a common denominator? → LCM (make bigger). When in doubt: GCF ≤ smallest number; LCM ≥ largest number.
Incomplete prime factorization — missing a prime factor, especially for larger numbers.
Fix: Always divide until you can't divide further. Use a factor tree systematically. Verify by multiplying your prime factors back: 2² × 3² × 5 = 4 × 9 × 5 = 180 ✓
Forgetting to use the correct exponent rule for GCF vs LCM.
Fix: Use this memory rule — GCF = "minimum" (take the lower power), LCM = "maximum" (take the higher power). Write out all prime factors with exponents side by side to compare visually.
Applying GCF/LCM to three or more numbers at once.
Fix: The method is the same. For GCF: take primes common to ALL numbers at the lowest exponent. For LCM: take all primes from ANY number at the highest exponent. Example — GCF(12, 18, 30): 12=2²×3, 18=2×3², 30=2×3×5 → GCF = 2¹×3¹ = 6.
Assuming GCF of two consecutive integers is always 1.
Fix: This is actually always true (consecutive integers share no common factor > 1), but it's worth knowing why: if n and n+1 shared a factor d > 1, then d would divide their difference (n+1) − n = 1, which is impossible.

Exponents & Roots (Including Fractional Exponents)

📋
Criterion 01
Description

An exponent tells you how many times a number (the base) is multiplied by itself. Written as bⁿ, where b is the base and n is the exponent (also called the power or index).

2³ = 2 × 2 × 2 = 8     (base = 2, exponent = 3)
5² = 5 × 5 = 25       (base = 5, exponent = 2, read: "5 squared")

A root is the inverse operation of an exponent. The nth root of a number x asks: "what value, raised to the power n, equals x?"

√9 = 3     because 3² = 9     (square root)
∛8 = 2     because 2³ = 8     (cube root)

A fractional exponent unifies both concepts: b^(m/n) = (ⁿ√b)^m. The denominator is the root, the numerator is the power. This is one of the most important bridges in algebra.

8^(1/3) = ∛8 = 2
27^(2/3) = (∛27)² = 3² = 9
🎯
Criterion 02
Purpose
AlgebraExponents express repeated multiplication compactly and are fundamental to polynomials, scientific notation, and growth models.
DataExponential functions model growth and decay — population growth, compound interest, radioactive decay, viral spread.
StatsStandard deviation uses a square root: σ = √(variance). Root operations appear in nearly every statistical formula.
MLFractional exponents are used in normalization, scaling, and activation functions. The gradient of x^n = n·x^(n−1) directly powers all calculus in ML.
CalcUnderstanding bⁿ with all exponent rules is a prerequisite for logarithms, calculus derivatives, and exponential models.
NormsVector norms in linear algebra use exponents: L2 norm = √(x₁² + x₂² + ... xₙ²) — the Euclidean distance formula.
⚙️
Criterion 03
How This Works

The 7 Core Exponent Laws

1. Product Rule:    bᵐ × bⁿ = b^(m+n)
   Example: 2³ × 2⁴ = 2⁷ = 128

2. Quotient Rule:   bᵐ ÷ bⁿ = b^(m−n)
   Example: 5⁶ ÷ 5² = 5⁴ = 625

3. Power Rule:     (bᵐ)ⁿ = b^(m×n)
   Example: (3²)⁴ = 3⁸ = 6561

4. Zero Exponent:   b⁰ = 1  (for any b ≠ 0)
   Example: 7⁰ = 1, (−5)⁰ = 1

5. Negative Exponent: b^(−n) = 1/bⁿ
   Example: 2^(−3) = 1/8

6. Fractional Exponent: b^(1/n) = ⁿ√b
   Example: 16^(1/4) = ⁴√16 = 2

7. General Fraction: b^(m/n) = (ⁿ√b)^m = ⁿ√(bᵐ)
   Example: 8^(2/3) = (∛8)² = 2² = 4

Root Rules

Product Rule:   √(a×b) = √a × √b
  Example: √(4×9) = √4 × √9 = 2 × 3 = 6 ✓ (√36 = 6)

Quotient Rule:  √(a/b) = √a / √b
  Example: √(25/4) = 5/2 = 2.5

Simplifying Roots: pull out perfect square factors
  Example: √72 = √(36×2) = √36 × √2 = 6√2

Order of evaluation with fractional exponents: Always compute the root first, then the power — it keeps numbers smaller and avoids overflow.

64^(5/6):  root first → ⁶√64 = 2 → then power → 2⁵ = 32 ✓
(vs. power first → 64⁵ = 1,073,741,824 → ⁶√ of that = 32 ✓ but harder)
🧪
Criterion 04
Sample Problems
Sample A — Applying Exponent Laws
Simplify: (2³ × 2⁵) ÷ 2⁴

Step 1 — Product Rule: 2³ × 2⁵ = 2^(3+5) = 2⁸
Step 2 — Quotient Rule: 2⁸ ÷ 2⁴ = 2^(8−4) = 2⁴
Step 3 — Evaluate: 2⁴ = 16
✔ Always combine using laws before evaluating — avoids large intermediate numbers.
Sample B — Negative Exponent
Simplify: 3^(−2) + 4^(−1)

3^(−2) = 1/3² = 1/9
4^(−1) = 1/4

1/9 + 1/4 → LCD = 36
= 4/36 + 9/36 = 13/36
✔ Negative exponents become fractions — then apply regular fraction rules.
Sample C — Fractional Exponent
Evaluate: 125^(2/3)

Step 1 — Identify: denominator = 3 (cube root), numerator = 2 (power)
Step 2 — Root first: ∛125 = 5   (because 5³ = 125)
Step 3 — Power: 5² = 25
✔ b^(m/n) → root first (denominator), then power (numerator).
Sample D — Simplify a Radical (Root)
Simplify: √180

180 = 36 × 5   (36 is a perfect square)
√180 = √(36 × 5) = √36 × √5 = 6√5
✔ Always look for the largest perfect square factor inside the radical.
Sample E — Data Analytics Application
A dataset has variance = 144.
What is the standard deviation?

σ = √variance = √144 = 12

If another dataset has variance = 50:
σ = √50 = √(25×2) = 5√2 ≈ 7.07
✔ Standard deviation is literally the square root of variance — one of the most used formulas in statistics.
⚠️
Criterion 05
Common Challenges & How to Solve Them
Misreading negative exponents — thinking b^(−n) = −bⁿ (negative value) instead of 1/bⁿ (fraction).
Fix: A negative exponent means reciprocal, NOT negative result. 2^(−3) = 1/8, not −8. A negative base with even exponent gives a positive result: (−3)² = 9. Only an odd exponent keeps the negative: (−3)³ = −27.
Adding or subtracting terms with exponents directly — e.g. treating 2³ + 2⁴ = 2⁷ (wrong!).
Fix: Exponent laws only apply to multiplication and division, NOT addition or subtraction. 2³ + 2⁴ = 8 + 16 = 24. You can factor: 2³(1 + 2) = 8 × 3 = 24, but you cannot add the exponents.
Confusing (ab)^n with ab^n — the parentheses matter critically.
Fix: (2×3)² = 6² = 36, but 2×3² = 2×9 = 18. When an exponent is outside parentheses, it applies to everything inside. Without parentheses, it applies only to the immediately preceding base.
Not simplifying before evaluating fractional exponents — resulting in massive numbers that are hard to root.
Fix: Always apply the root (denominator) first. For 32^(3/5): compute ⁵√32 = 2 first, then 2³ = 8. Much easier than 32³ = 32768 then ⁵√32768.
Assuming √(a + b) = √a + √b — one of the most common algebra errors.
Fix: This is FALSE. √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5, but √9 + √16 = 3 + 4 = 7 ≠ 5. The product rule (√ab = √a·√b) works for multiplication ONLY, never for addition or subtraction inside a radical.
Zero and one as bases or exponents — edge cases that trip people up.
Fix: Remember these: 0⁰ is undefined (or 1 by convention in some contexts). 0^n = 0 for any n > 0. 1^n = 1 always. b⁰ = 1 for b ≠ 0. b¹ = b always.
