Pre-Algebra · Subtopic 01

Scientific Notation

A standardized way to express very large or very small numbers compactly and precisely.
📋
Criterion 01
Description

Scientific notation is a way of writing numbers as a product of two parts: a coefficient and a power of 10. The standard form is:

a × 10ⁿ where: 1 ≤ |a| < 10 (the coefficient — always one non-zero digit before the decimal) n = any integer (positive, negative, or zero)

If the number is large (≥ 10), the exponent n is positive. If the number is small (between 0 and 1), the exponent n is negative.

Large: 93,000,000 = 9.3 × 10⁷ Small: 0.000045 = 4.5 × 10⁻⁵ Normal: 620 = 6.2 × 10²

Scientific notation is built directly on exponents and place value. Moving the decimal point right increases the exponent; moving it left decreases it.

🎯
Criterion 02
Purpose
ReadabilityAvoids writing out dozens of zeros. Comparing 3.0 × 10²³ to 6.0 × 10²³ is far clearer than comparing their full digit forms.
Data ScaleIn data analytics, datasets can have values ranging from nanoseconds (10⁻⁹) to petabytes (10¹⁵). Scientific notation keeps these manageable in calculations and displays.
PrecisionIt explicitly shows significant figures — making the precision of a measurement unambiguous. 4.50 × 10³ shows 3 significant figures; 4500 does not clarify this.
ComputingComputers and calculators use scientific notation (floating-point representation) internally. Understanding it helps interpret output like 1.23e+8 or 5.6e-4 in Python/R/Excel.
ML / StatsLoss values, learning rates, and probability values in machine learning often appear as very small decimals — reading 1.5e-7 correctly is an everyday data skill.
⚙️
Criterion 03
How This Works

Converting a standard number → scientific notation

1
Move the decimal point until only one non-zero digit sits to its left. This gives you the coefficient a.
2
Count how many places you moved the decimal. That count becomes the exponent n.
3
If you moved the decimal left (large number), exponent is positive. If you moved it right (small number), exponent is negative.
0.000307 → move decimal right 4 places → 3.07 × 10⁻⁴ 56,800 → move decimal left 4 places → 5.68 × 10⁴

Arithmetic with Scientific Notation

OperationRuleExample
MultiplyMultiply coefficients, ADD exponents(3×10⁴)(2×10³) = 6×10⁷
DivideDivide coefficients, SUBTRACT exponents(8×10⁶)÷(2×10²) = 4×10⁴
Add/SubtractMake exponents equal first, then add/subtract coefficients3×10⁴ + 2×10³ = 3×10⁴ + 0.2×10⁴ = 3.2×10⁴
PowerRaise coefficient to power, MULTIPLY exponents(2×10³)² = 4×10⁶

Reading computer/calculator output:

Python/R output: 1.23e+8 means 1.23 × 10⁸ = 123,000,000 5.6e-4 means 5.6 × 10⁻⁴ = 0.00056 Excel output: 1.23E+08 — same format, capital E

Criterion 04
Sample Problems
Sample A — Convert to Scientific Notation
Write 0.00000892 in scientific notation. Step 1: Move decimal right until one non-zero digit is left of it. 0.00000892 → 8.92 (moved 6 places right) Step 2: Moving right → negative exponent Result: 8.92 × 10⁻⁶
✔ Check: 8.92 × 10⁻⁶ = 8.92 ÷ 1,000,000 = 0.00000892 ✓
Sample B — Convert FROM Scientific Notation
Write 7.04 × 10⁵ as a standard number. 10⁵ = 100,000 → move decimal 5 places RIGHT 7.04 → 704,000 Result: 704,000
✔ Positive exponent = large number = move decimal right.
Sample C — Multiply in Scientific Notation
(4.5 × 10⁶) × (3.0 × 10⁻²) Step 1 — Coefficients: 4.5 × 3.0 = 13.5 Step 2 — Exponents: 10⁶ × 10⁻² = 10^(6+(−2)) = 10⁴ Step 3 — Combine: 13.5 × 10⁴ Step 4 — Adjust: 13.5 is not in [1,10) → 1.35 × 10¹ × 10⁴ = 1.35 × 10⁵ Result: 1.35 × 10⁵ = 135,000
✔ Always re-adjust after multiplying if the coefficient goes outside [1, 10).
Sample D — Data Analytics Context
A dataset has 2,400,000 rows. Each row uses 512 bytes of memory. Total memory = 2,400,000 × 512 bytes In scientific notation: 2,400,000 = 2.4 × 10⁶ 512 = 5.12 × 10² Multiply: (2.4 × 5.12) × 10^(6+2) = 12.288 × 10⁸ = 1.2288 × 10⁹ bytes ≈ 1.23 GB
✔ Scientific notation makes memory/storage estimation fast and readable.
⚠️
Criterion 05
Common Challenges & How to Solve Them
Moving the decimal in the wrong direction — making a large number small or vice versa.
Fix: Use this anchor: large number (≥10) → positive exponent → moved decimal LEFT. Small number (0 < x < 1) → negative exponent → moved decimal RIGHT. After converting, always verify by mentally expanding: 3.5 × 10³ should give thousands-range, not decimals.
Forgetting to re-adjust the coefficient after multiplication or division — leaving a result like 14.7 × 10⁵.
Fix: After any arithmetic, always check: is the coefficient between 1 and 10 (not including 10)? If not, adjust. 14.7 × 10⁵ = 1.47 × 10⁶. Rule: for each step you move the decimal right in the coefficient, subtract 1 from the exponent, and vice versa.
Adding/subtracting without matching exponents first — incorrect result.
Fix: You MUST equalize exponents before adding or subtracting coefficients. Convert both to the same power of 10 first. Example: 4.2×10³ + 3.1×10² → convert to same exponent → 4.2×10³ + 0.31×10³ = 4.51×10³. Think of it like adding km and m — you must convert units first.
Misreading e-notation from Python/R — confusing 1e-3 as 1 to the power of -3.
Fix: In code output, "e" means "× 10^". So 1e-3 = 1 × 10⁻³ = 0.001. It is NOT 1³ or -3 or anything else. This is floating-point notation and is identical to scientific notation. Practice converting a few: 2.5e4 = 25,000 · 6e-7 = 0.0000006.
Significant figures confusion — treating all digits in a standard number as significant.
Fix: Scientific notation removes ambiguity. 3,400 could mean 2, 3, or 4 significant figures. In scientific notation: 3.4×10³ (2 sig figs), 3.40×10³ (3 sig figs), 3.400×10³ (4 sig figs). The coefficient always shows exactly how many digits are significant.

Variables, Expressions, and Equations

The transition from arithmetic to algebra — where symbols replace specific numbers and relationships become general rules.
📋
Criterion 01
Description

A variable is a symbol — usually a letter — that represents an unknown or changeable value. Variables allow us to write general rules instead of specific calculations.

x, y, n, a, b → common variable names x = 5 means "the variable x holds the value 5" x can represent any value until it is defined or solved for

An expression is a combination of variables, numbers, and operations — but with no equals sign. It has a value but poses no question. It cannot be "solved" — only simplified or evaluated.

3x + 7 → algebraic expression 2a² − 5b + 1 → expression with two variables 4(x + 3) → expression with grouping

An equation is a statement that two expressions are equal, indicated by an equals sign (=). An equation can be solved — finding the value(s) of the variable that make it true.

3x + 7 = 22 → linear equation (one variable) 2a + b = 10 → equation with two variables x² − 5x + 6 = 0 → quadratic equation KEY DISTINCTION: Expression: 3x + 7 (no = sign, cannot be solved) Equation: 3x + 7 = 22 (has = sign, can be solved → x = 5)
🎯
Criterion 02
Purpose
FoundationThis is the gateway to all of algebra. Every formula, function, model, and algorithm in mathematics uses variables and equations. Nothing above this level works without it.
ModelingVariables let you represent unknown or changing quantities — dataset columns, model parameters, input features — as general symbols that apply to any value.
DataIn data analytics, every column in a table is a variable. Every formula in Excel or SQL is an expression. Every constraint in optimization is an equation or inequality.
MLA machine learning model is fundamentally a collection of equations: ŷ = w₁x₁ + w₂x₂ + b. Variables are features (xᵢ), parameters (wᵢ, b), and predictions (ŷ). Understanding this is step zero of ML mathematics.
LogicWriting and solving equations is how you find unknowns from known relationships — the core activity in data-driven problem solving.
⚙️
Criterion 03
How This Works

Part 1 — Evaluating Expressions
Substitute a value for each variable, then follow order of operations (PEMDAS).

Expression: 4x² − 3x + 1, evaluate at x = 2 Step 1: substitute → 4(2)² − 3(2) + 1 Step 2: exponent → 4(4) − 3(2) + 1 Step 3: multiply → 16 − 6 + 1 Step 4: left→right → 11

Part 2 — Simplifying Expressions
Combine like terms (same variable and same exponent) and apply the distributive property.

Like terms: 5x + 3y − 2x + 7y = 3x + 10y (combine x-terms and y-terms separately) Distribution: 3(2x − 4) = 6x − 12 (multiply 3 into each term inside the parentheses) Expand & simplify: 2(x + 3) + 4(x − 1) = 2x + 6 + 4x − 4 = 6x + 2

Part 3 — Solving Linear Equations
The goal: isolate the variable on one side using inverse operations. Whatever you do to one side, you MUST do to the other.

1
Distribute and simplify both sides fully.
2
Move all variable terms to one side using addition or subtraction.
3
Move all constants to the other side.
4
Divide (or multiply) both sides to isolate the variable.
5
Verify: substitute your answer back into the original equation.
Solve: 5(x − 2) = 3x + 4 Step 1: distribute → 5x − 10 = 3x + 4 Step 2: move vars → 5x − 3x = 4 + 10 Step 3: simplify → 2x = 14 Step 4: divide → x = 7 Verify: 5(7−2) = 5(5) = 25 and 3(7)+4 = 21+4 = 25 ✓

Types of equations by solution:

One solution: x + 3 = 7 → x = 4 (most common) No solution: x + 3 = x + 5 → 3 = 5 (contradiction, impossible) Infinite solutions: 2(x+1) = 2x+2 → 2 = 2 (identity, always true)
🧪
Criterion 04
Sample Problems
Sample A — Evaluating an Expression
Evaluate 3a² + 2b − ab at a = 4, b = −1 Substitute: 3(4)² + 2(−1) − (4)(−1) Exponent: 3(16) + 2(−1) − (4)(−1) Multiply: 48 + (−2) − (−4) Simplify: 48 − 2 + 4 = 50
✔ Note: subtracting a negative = adding a positive. −(−4) = +4.
Sample B — Simplifying an Expression
Simplify: 4(3x − 2) − 2(x + 5) + 7x Step 1 — Distribute: 12x − 8 − 2x − 10 + 7x Step 2 — Group x-terms: (12x − 2x + 7x) + (−8 − 10) Step 3 — Combine: 17x + (−18) Result: 17x − 18
✔ You can only combine terms with the exact same variable and exponent (like terms).
Sample C — Solving a Linear Equation
Solve: 3(2x + 1) − 4 = 2(x + 5) + 3 Left side: 6x + 3 − 4 = 6x − 1 Right side: 2x + 10 + 3 = 2x + 13 Equation: 6x − 1 = 2x + 13 Move vars: 6x − 2x = 13 + 1 Simplify: 4x = 14 Divide: x = 14/4 = 7/2 = 3.5 Verify: Left: 3(2×3.5 + 1) − 4 = 3(8) − 4 = 20 Right: 2(3.5 + 5) + 3 = 2(8.5) + 3 = 20 ✓
✔ Always verify by substituting back — this catches arithmetic errors instantly.
Sample D — Writing an Equation from a Word Problem
"A data analyst earns a base salary of $3,000/month plus $150 for each report completed. Last month she earned $4,350. How many reports did she complete?" Let r = number of reports. Equation: 3000 + 150r = 4350 Solve: 150r = 4350 − 3000 150r = 1350 r = 9 She completed 9 reports. Verify: 3000 + 150(9) = 3000 + 1350 = 4350 ✓
✔ Translating words → equation is one of the most important real-world algebra skills.
Sample E — ML Equation Connection
Linear regression model: ŷ = w·x + b Given: w = 2.5, b = −1.3 Find ŷ when x = 4. ŷ = 2.5(4) + (−1.3) = 10 − 1.3 = 8.7 This is just evaluating an algebraic expression — the exact same skill as Sample A, applied to ML.
✔ Every ML prediction is an expression evaluation. Understanding this connects algebra directly to data science.
⚠️
Criterion 05
Common Challenges & How to Solve Them
Confusing expressions and equations — trying to "solve" an expression or forgetting you need an equals sign to solve.
Fix: Simple check — does it have an = sign? Yes → equation (solve it). No → expression (simplify or evaluate it). You cannot find a specific value of x from 3x + 7 alone — you need 3x + 7 = something.
Incorrect distribution — especially with negatives: −2(x − 3) written as −2x − 6 instead of −2x + 6.
Fix: The negative sign multiplies EVERY term inside. −2(x − 3) = (−2)(x) + (−2)(−3) = −2x + 6. Write each multiplication explicitly until it becomes automatic. Negative × negative = positive is the rule to internalize.
Combining unlike terms — adding 3x + 5x² and getting 8x².
Fix: Like terms must share the same variable AND the same exponent. 3x and 5x² are different terms — one is linear, one is quadratic. They cannot be combined. Think of them as different "types" of objects: you can't add apples and oranges. 3x + 5x² stays as 3x + 5x².
Performing an operation to only one side of an equation — breaking the balance.
Fix: An equation is a balance scale. Whatever you do to one side, you must do identically to the other. If you add 5 to the left, add 5 to the right. If you divide the left by 3, divide the right by 3. Writing each step on a new line and labeling the operation helps enforce this habit.
Not verifying the solution — getting the wrong answer and not catching it.
Fix: Always substitute your answer back into the original equation (not a simplified form — the original). If both sides are equal, you're correct. This takes 10 seconds and catches nearly every arithmetic mistake. Build it into every solve as a non-negotiable last step.
Forgetting that equations can have no solution or infinite solutions — assuming every equation has exactly one answer.
Fix: If your solving process results in a false statement (e.g., 3 = 7), the equation has NO solution. If it results in a true identity (e.g., 0 = 0), it has INFINITE solutions. These are valid mathematical outcomes, not mistakes. Recognize them by what remains after the variable cancels out.
