Fractions, Decimals & Percentages

Three representations of the same mathematical idea — parts of a whole. Converting between them and operating on them precisely is the bedrock of all quantitative analysis.
1
Criterion 01

Description

What fractions, decimals, and percentages are, and how they relate to each other.

A fraction is a way of expressing a part of a whole using two integers: the numerator (top — how many parts you have) and the denominator (bottom — how many equal parts the whole is divided into). The fraction a/b means: divide the whole into b equal parts and take a of them.

A decimal is a number expressed in base-10 positional notation, where digits to the right of the decimal point represent tenths, hundredths, thousandths, etc. Decimals are just fractions written with denominators that are powers of 10.

A percentage is a special fraction with denominator 100 — "per cent" literally means "per hundred." It expresses a number as parts out of 100, making comparisons intuitive across different-sized quantities.

-- The three are the same number, different notation: 3/4 = 0.75 = 75% ← all represent "three-quarters" 1/3 ≈ 0.333... ≈ 33.3% 7/5 = 1.4 = 140% ← fractions and % can exceed 1 -- Anatomy of a fraction: Proper fraction: a/b where |a| < |b| → value between −1 and 1 example: 3/7, 5/8 Improper fraction: a/b where |a| ≥ |b| → |value| ≥ 1 example: 9/4, 13/5 Mixed number: whole part + fraction → 2 and 1/4 = 9/4 -- Types of decimals: Terminating: 0.25, 0.5, 1.375 ← fraction with denominator 2ⁿ·5ᵐ Repeating: 0.333..., 0.142857142857... ← all other fractions Irrational: √2 = 1.41421356... ← cannot be expressed as a fraction

2
Criterion 02

Purpose

Why fractions, decimals, and percentages are foundational to all data work.
MetricsEvery data metric involves fractions: accuracy = correct/total, precision = TP/(TP+FP), click-through rate = clicks/impressions, conversion rate = conversions/visitors. These are fractions expressed as decimals or percentages depending on context. You cannot interpret or report any metric without understanding all three forms.
ProbabilitiesProbabilities are fractions between 0 and 1. P(A) = favorable outcomes / total outcomes. Expressing probability as 0.35, 35%, or 7/20 are all equivalent — a data scientist must move fluidly between these representations. The choice of notation affects how formulas are written and computed.
Growth RatesPercentage change = (new − old) / old × 100 is the universal language of growth reporting in business analytics. Revenue grew 12.5%. Churn decreased by 8%. Model accuracy improved from 84% to 91% — a 7 percentage point increase, which is an 8.33% relative improvement (different from the absolute increase).
NormalizationNormalizing data — scaling features to [0,1] or [−1,1] — is fundamentally dividing by a reference quantity to express everything as fractions. Min-max normalization gives a fraction: (x−min)/(max−min). Understanding that this produces a value between 0 and 1 comes from fraction arithmetic.
Weighted AvgWeighted averages combine values using fractional weights that must sum to 1 (i.e., 100%). Model ensembles, portfolio returns, student grade calculations — all use weighted fractions. Understanding that weights are fractions summing to 1 is the conceptual basis for all weighted combination operations.
PrecisionFloating-point numbers in computers are fractions in binary. Rounding errors, truncation, and precision loss are all fraction/decimal concepts. A data engineer who understands that 0.1 cannot be represented exactly in binary (it's a repeating fraction in base 2) knows why floating-point arithmetic can produce surprising results.

How This Works

Conversions, operations on fractions, and percentage calculations — all with worked examples.
A
Converting Between Forms
The most fundamental skill — moving fluently between all three representations.
-- Fraction → Decimal: divide numerator by denominator 3/8 = 3 ÷ 8 = 0.375 5/6 = 5 ÷ 6 = 0.8333... -- Decimal → Fraction: use place value, then simplify 0.6 = 6/10 = 3/5 ← tenths place 0.35 = 35/100 = 7/20 ← hundredths place 0.125 = 125/1000 = 1/8 ← thousandths place 0.333... = 1/3 ← repeating: let x = 0.333..., then 10x = 3.333..., 10x − x = 3, 9x = 3, x = 3/9 = 1/3 -- Decimal → Percentage: multiply by 100 0.75 × 100 = 75% 0.035 × 100 = 3.5% 1.4 × 100 = 140% -- Percentage → Decimal: divide by 100 68% ÷ 100 = 0.68 0.5% ÷ 100 = 0.005 250% ÷ 100 = 2.5 -- Fraction → Percentage: (fraction) × 100 3/4 × 100 = 75% 7/8 × 100 = 87.5%
B
Fraction Arithmetic — All Four Operations
Add/subtract need common denominators. Multiply/divide work directly.
-- Addition & Subtraction: find LCD, make equivalent fractions, add/subtract numerators 2/3 + 1/4: LCD = 12 = 8/12 + 3/12 = 11/12 5/6 − 3/8: LCD = 24 = 20/24 − 9/24 = 11/24 -- Multiplication: multiply numerators, multiply denominators, simplify 3/5 × 4/7 = (3×4)/(5×7) = 12/35 (Cancel before multiplying when possible: 3/4 × 8/9 = 3/4 × 8/9 = (3×8)/(4×9) = 24/36 = 2/3 OR cancel first: 3/4 × 8/9 = (3÷3)/(9÷3) × (8÷4)/(4÷4) = 1/1 × 2/3 = 2/3 ← faster) -- Division: multiply by the reciprocal of the second fraction (Keep-Change-Flip) 3/4 ÷ 2/5 = 3/4 × 5/2 = 15/8 = 1 and 7/8 5/6 ÷ 5 = 5/6 × 1/5 = 5/30 = 1/6
C
Percentage Calculations — The Six Standard Types
Every percentage problem in data analytics fits one of these six templates.
-- Type 1: Find % of a value "What is 35% of 240?" 240 × 0.35 = 84 -- Type 2: Find what % one number is of another "18 is what % of 72?" 18/72 × 100 = 0.25 × 100 = 25% -- Type 3: Find the original value "65 is 40% of what?" 65 / 0.40 = 162.5 -- Type 4: Percentage increase % increase = (new − old) / old × 100 Old = 80, New = 92: (92−80)/80 × 100 = 12/80 × 100 = 15% -- Type 5: Percentage decrease % decrease = (old − new) / old × 100 Old = 150, New = 120: (150−120)/150 × 100 = 30/150 × 100 = 20% -- Type 6: Find value after % change Original × (1 + rate) for increase Original × (1 − rate) for decrease $400 after 15% increase: 400 × 1.15 = $460 $400 after 15% decrease: 400 × 0.85 = $340
⚠️
Critical distinction — Percentage points vs Percentage change: If accuracy goes from 80% to 88%, it increased by 8 percentage points (absolute difference). But the percentage change is (88−80)/80 × 100 = 10% (relative). These are different numbers and both matter in reporting. Using one when you mean the other is a common and consequential error in data presentations.
Simplify a Fraction
a/b → (a÷GCF)/(b÷GCF)
Divide both by GCF. 12/18: GCF=6 → 2/3.

x = 0.d̄, 10x−x=d → x=d/9
0.7̄: 9x=7, x=7/9. For 0.12̄: 9x=1.2-0.1=1.1, etc.

a b/c = (ac+b)/c
2 3/4 = (2×4+3)/4 = 11/4. Reverse: divide to get quotient & remainder.
% of a Number
P% of N = N × (P/100)
35% of 80 = 80 × 0.35 = 28.

A model ensemble combines three classifiers: Model A has weight 1/2, Model B has weight 1/3, and Model C has weight 1/6. Verify weights sum to 1, then compute the weighted prediction when A predicts 0.9, B predicts 0.6, and C predicts 0.75.

S3Percentage change, absolute vs relativeBusiness Analytics
A product's sales were $240,000 last quarter and $276,000 this quarter. A model's F1 score improved from 0.72 to 0.79. Compute and distinguish absolute change, percentage change, and — for the model — the percentage point change.
SALES: Absolute change: 276,000 − 240,000 = $36,000 Percentage change: 36,000 / 240,000 × 100 = 15% New as % of old: 276,000 / 240,000 × 100 = 115% MODEL F1 SCORE: Absolute change (percentage points): 0.79 − 0.72 = 0.07 In "percentage point" language: improved by 7 percentage points Relative % change: (0.79 − 0.72) / 0.72 × 100 = 0.07/0.72 × 100 ≈ 9.72% CRITICAL DISTINCTION: "F1 improved by 7 percentage points" — absolute, 0.72→0.79 "F1 improved by 9.72%" — relative, how much of 0.72 Both are true. They measure different things. In reporting, state which you mean — they differ by nearly 3×! If a competitor said "our model improved by 10%": That could mean 0.80 → 0.88 (8 pp absolute, 10% relative) OR 0.72 → 0.79 (7 pp absolute, 9.72% relative) — very different!
Takeaway: Percentage points and percentage change are different measurements. Absolute percentage-point change is straightforward subtraction. Relative percentage change is (new−old)/old × 100. Always specify which one you are reporting — the difference can make results appear 3× better or worse than they are.
S4Weighted average GPA and grade calculationApplied Math
A student takes 4 courses with the following grades and credit hours: Math (4 credits, A = 4.0), Statistics (3 credits, B+ = 3.3), Programming (3 credits, A− = 3.7), Writing (2 credits, B = 3.0). Compute the weighted GPA.
Weights are credit hours expressed as fractions of total credits: Total credits = 4 + 3 + 3 + 2 = 12 Weighted GPA = Σ (credits × grade) / total credits Numerator: 4 × 4.0 = 16.0 3 × 3.3 = 9.9 3 × 3.7 = 11.1 2 × 3.0 = 6.0 Total = 43.0 GPA = 43.0 / 12 = 3.583... As a fraction: 43/12 ≈ 3.58 (rounded to 2 decimal places) As percentage of max (4.0): 3.583/4.0 × 100 = 89.6% Verify using fractional weights: Weights: 4/12, 3/12, 3/12, 2/12 = 1/3, 1/4, 1/4, 1/6 GPA = (1/3)(4.0) + (1/4)(3.3) + (1/4)(3.7) + (1/6)(3.0) = 1.333 + 0.825 + 0.925 + 0.500 = 3.583 ✓
Takeaway: Weighted averages use fractional weights (credits/total credits) that sum to 1. This exact structure — Σ(wᵢ × xᵢ) where Σwᵢ=1 — appears in machine learning (attention mechanisms, ensemble weights, expected values). GPA is the simplest, most relatable instance of a weighted expectation.
S5Floating-point fraction precision — data engineering contextData Engineering
A data engineer notices that 0.1 + 0.2 in Python returns 0.30000000000000004 instead of 0.3. Explain why, using the concept of repeating fractions in base 2.
In base 10: 0.1 = 1/10 — terminating decimal In base 2: 1/10 = ? Convert 0.1 to binary: 0.1 × 2 = 0.2 → 0 0.2 × 2 = 0.4 → 0 0.4 × 2 = 0.8 → 0 0.8 × 2 = 1.6 → 1 0.6 × 2 = 1.2 → 1 0.2 × 2 = 0.4 → 0 ← repeats! 0.1 in base 2 = 0.0001100110011... (repeating) Just like 1/3 = 0.333... in base 10, 1/10 is a repeating fraction in base 2. Computers store a finite approximation → rounding error. Result: 0.1 (stored) ≈ 0.1000000000000000055511151... 0.2 (stored) ≈ 0.2000000000000000111022302... Sum ≈ 0.3000000000000000444089210... Displayed as: 0.30000000000000004 Fix in Python: from decimal import Decimal Decimal('0.1') + Decimal('0.2') → Decimal('0.3') ← exact! # or: round(0.1 + 0.2, 10) → 0.3
Takeaway: Floating-point precision errors are repeating-fraction problems in base 2. A number that terminates in base 10 may repeat in base 2 — just as 1/3 repeats in base 10. Understanding fractions and decimal representations is why this happens and how to fix it — critical for financial calculations where exact decimal arithmetic is required.

Ratios and Proportions

The mathematics of relative comparison and scaled equality — the language of rates, scaling, and every model that relates two measurable quantities.
1
Criterion 01

Description

What ratios and proportions are, how to write them, and what they express.

A ratio is a comparison of two (or more) quantities by division. It tells you the relative size of one quantity compared to another. Unlike a fraction, which expresses a part-of-whole relationship, a ratio can compare any two quantities — even ones with different units (making it a rate).

A proportion is a statement that two ratios are equal. It is the mathematical way of saying "these two fractions represent the same relationship," and it is the basis for all scaling, similarity, and cross-multiplication reasoning.

-- Ratio notation (three equivalent ways to write the same ratio): a to b = a : b = a/b Ratio of 15 to 20: 15 to 20 = 15:20 = 15/20 = 3/4 (simplified) Always simplify by dividing both parts by their GCF. -- Types of ratios: Part-to-Part: boys to girls = 3:4 (neither is the total) Part-to-Whole: boys to students = 3:7 (one is the total — this is a fraction) Rate: 60 miles per hour = 60 miles : 1 hour (different units) -- Proportion: two equal ratios a/b = c/d ← "a is to b as c is to d" Cross-multiplication property: if a/b = c/d, then a·d = b·c Example: 3/4 = 12/16 check: 3×16 = 48, 4×12 = 48 ✓
⚡
Rate vs ratio: A rate is a ratio with different units — miles per hour, cost per unit, clicks per impression, errors per thousand lines of code. All rates are ratios, but not all ratios are rates. In data analytics, most KPIs are rates: revenue per customer, conversion rate, cost per acquisition. Understanding them as ratios makes their arithmetic transparent.

A unit rate is a ratio with a denominator of 1 — it expresses "per one unit." It is the most interpretable form: $3.50 per kg, 45 conversions per day, 95.2% accuracy per epoch. Converting any rate to a unit rate is the act of dividing to make the denominator exactly 1.

-- Direct vs Inverse proportion: Direct proportion: y = kx → as x increases, y increases proportionally y₁/x₁ = y₂/x₂ = k (constant) Example: cost = $5 × quantity (more items → more cost) Inverse proportion: y = k/x → as x increases, y decreases x₁y₁ = x₂y₂ = k (constant) Example: speed × time = distance (faster → less time)
Purpose
2
Criterion 02

Purpose

Why ratios and proportions appear throughout analytics, modeling, and data engineering.
KPIs & RatesEvery key performance indicator in business analytics is a rate: conversion rate, click-through rate, churn rate, retention rate, error rate. All are ratios expressed as fractions or percentages. Being able to manipulate, compare, and scale ratios is the daily work of a data analyst.
ScalingProportions are the mathematics of scaling. When you resize an image, scale a feature, normalize data, or resize a dataset — you are maintaining a proportion. A model trained on a 70/30 train/test split uses a proportional allocation. Map scales, model scales, and data sampling all rely on proportional reasoning.
Cross-ValidationStratified sampling in cross-validation maintains the ratio of class labels across all folds. If the dataset is 80% class A and 20% class B, each fold should reflect that 4:1 ratio. This is proportional reasoning applied to data partitioning to prevent sampling bias.
SimilarityCosine similarity — one of the most used similarity measures in NLP and recommendation systems — computes the ratio of the dot product to the product of vector magnitudes. It is a normalized ratio that equals 1 for identical vectors and 0 for orthogonal ones. Understanding it as a ratio explains why it is bounded.
Unit AnalysisDimensional analysis — tracking units through calculations — is ratio arithmetic. Converting km/h to m/s, dollars per share to total portfolio value, or bytes to gigabytes all require multiplying and dividing ratios. Unit analysis prevents order-of-magnitude errors that can silently corrupt data pipelines.
BayesianBayes' theorem works with ratios of probabilities. The likelihood ratio P(evidence|H₁) / P(evidence|H₀) measures how much more likely evidence is under one hypothesis than another. The posterior odds = prior odds × likelihood ratio. This is proportion and ratio applied to probabilistic reasoning.

Criterion 03

How This Works

Simplifying ratios, solving proportions, scaling, unit rates, and direct vs inverse proportion.
A
Simplifying Ratios & Computing Unit Rates
Express as simplest form and as a per-one-unit rate.
-- Simplify by dividing both parts by their GCF: 24:36 → GCF=12 → 2:3 150:200:250 → GCF=50 → 3:4:5 -- Three-part ratio splitting a whole: Share $3,600 in ratio 3:4:5 Total parts = 3+4+5 = 12 Part value = 3600/12 = $300 per part Shares: 3×300=$900, 4×300=$1200, 5×300=$1500 Check: 900+1200+1500 = 3600 ✓ -- Unit rate (make denominator = 1): Travel 270 km in 3 hours → 270/3 = 90 km/h Earn $480 in 6 days → 480/6 = $80/day 15 defects in 500 units → 15/500 = 0.03 defects/unit = 3%
B
Solving Proportions — Cross-Multiplication Method
Find the missing value when two ratios are set equal.
1
Write the proportion as two equal fractions: a/b = c/d.
2
Cross-multiply: a × d = b × c.
3
Solve the resulting linear equation for the unknown.
4
Verify: substitute back and check both fractions are equal.
-- Basic proportion: 3/4 = x/20 Cross-multiply: 3×20 = 4×x → 60 = 4x → x = 15 Check: 3/4 = 15/20 = 3/4 ✓ -- Real-world proportion (scaling a recipe): A recipe for 6 servings uses 2.5 cups of flour. How much flour for 10 servings? 6/2.5 = 10/x → 6x = 25 → x = 25/6 ≈ 4.17 cups -- Map scale proportion: Map scale 1:50,000. A distance is 3.4 cm on the map. What is the actual distance? 1/50000 = 3.4/d → d = 3.4 × 50000 = 170,000 cm = 1.7 km
C
Direct & Inverse Proportion — Identifying and Solving
Understand which type of proportional relationship a problem describes before solving.
Direct Proportion
y = kx   →   y/x = k
As x doubles, y doubles. Graph is a straight line through origin. k = constant of proportionality.
Inverse Proportion
y = k/x   →   xy = k
As x doubles, y halves. Graph is a hyperbola. Product is always constant.
-- Direct proportion example: A pump fills 120 liters in 4 minutes. How long for 300 liters? Volume ∝ time → 120/4 = 300/t → t = 300×4/120 = 10 minutes OR: unit rate = 30 liters/min → 300/30 = 10 min -- Inverse proportion example: 8 workers complete a job in 6 days. How long for 12 workers? workers × days = constant → 8×6 = 12×d → 48 = 12d → d = 4 days (More workers → fewer days — classic inverse proportion) -- How to identify the type: Direct: "more of X → more of Y at the same rate" Inverse: "more of X → less of Y to maintain the same total"
D
Ratio Splitting & Proportional Allocation
Distributing a total into parts that maintain a given ratio — used constantly in data partitioning.
1
Sum the ratio parts to find the total number of shares.
2
Divide the total by the sum of ratio parts to find the value of one share.
3
Multiply each ratio part by one share's value.
4
Verify: all shares should sum to the original total.
A dataset of 10,000 rows is split train:val:test = 7:1.5:1.5 Sum of parts = 7 + 1.5 + 1.5 = 10 One share = 10,000 / 10 = 1,000 rows Train: 7 × 1,000 = 7,000 rows (70%) Val: 1.5 × 1,000 = 1,500 rows (15%) Test: 1.5 × 1,000 = 1,500 rows (15%) Check: 7,000 + 1,500 + 1,500 = 10,000 ✓ Proportional fractions: Train = 7/10 = 0.7, Val = 1.5/10 = 0.15, Test = 0.15 These are fractions of the total — fraction and ratio in one formula.

S1Simplify, split, and solve a proportionPure Math
A bag contains red, blue, and green marbles in the ratio 5:3:2. There are 80 marbles total. How many of each color? If 10 more green marbles are added, what is the new simplified ratio?
Part 1 — Split 80 marbles in ratio 5:3:2: Total parts = 5+3+2 = 10 One part = 80/10 = 8 marbles Red: 5×8 = 40 Blue: 3×8 = 24 Green: 2×8 = 16 Check: 40+24+16 = 80 ✓ Part 2 — Add 10 green marbles: New totals: Red=40, Blue=24, Green=26 New ratio: 40:24:26 GCF(40,24,26) = 2 Simplified: 20:12:13 Check: 20+12+13=45 parts × (90/45)=2 per part 2×20=40, 2×12=24, 2×13=26 ✓
Takeaway: Ratio splitting is the core operation for allocating any fixed total into proportional parts. After modifying the quantities, always re-simplify by finding the new GCF. The simplified ratio (20:12:13) cannot be reduced further since GCF(20,12,13)=1.
S2Train/val/test split and class balanceData Analytics / ML
A dataset has 12,500 rows: 8,000 class 0 and 4,500 class 1. Split into train/val/test = 60:20:20. Maintain the class ratio in each split (stratified sampling). Find the exact number of each class in each split.
Step 1 — Split sizes (ratio 60:20:20 = 3:1:1): Total parts = 5. One part = 12,500/5 = 2,500 Train: 3×2,500 = 7,500 | Val: 1×2,500 = 2,500 | Test: 2,500 Step 2 — Class ratio in full dataset: Class 0: 8,000/12,500 = 0.64 = 64% Class 1: 4,500/12,500 = 0.36 = 36% Ratio = 8:4.5 → simplify → 16:9 Step 3 — Apply class ratio within each split: Train (7,500 total): Class 0: 7,500 × 0.64 = 4,800 Class 1: 7,500 × 0.36 = 2,700 Check: 4,800+2,700 = 7,500 ✓ Val (2,500 total): Class 0: 2,500 × 0.64 = 1,600 Class 1: 2,500 × 0.36 = 900 Test (2,500 total): Class 0: 2,500 × 0.64 = 1,600 Class 1: 2,500 × 0.36 = 900 Full check: (4,800+1,600+1,600)=8,000 Class 0 ✓ (2,700+900+900)=4,500 Class 1 ✓
Takeaway: Stratified sampling maintains the class ratio across all splits — this is proportional allocation applied to datasets. If you don't stratify, a split might end up with very different class distributions than the original, causing biased training and misleading evaluation. This is ratio reasoning in daily ML practice.
S3Unit rate analysis — cost per acquisitionBusiness Analytics
A marketing team runs three campaigns: Campaign A spent $12,000 and acquired 480 customers. Campaign B spent $8,500 and acquired 340 customers. Campaign C spent $15,600 and acquired 520 customers. Compute the CPA (cost per acquisition) for each, rank them, and determine how many customers Campaign A would acquire if given Campaign C's budget at its own CPA rate.
Cost Per Acquisition (unit rate: $/customer): A: 12,000/480 = $25.00 per customer B: 8,500/340 = $25.00 per customer C: 15,600/520 = $30.00 per customer Ranking (best CPA first): A = B ($25) > C ($30) Campaigns A and B are equally efficient. Campaign C costs 20% more per customer than A or B. If Campaign A gets Campaign C's budget ($15,600): At CPA of $25: customers = 15,600/25 = 624 Percentage more customers than C at same budget: (624 − 520)/520 × 100 = 104/520 × 100 = 20% Campaign A would acquire 20% more customers than C for the same spend. Alternative check (proportion): A's budget:A's customers = C's budget:x 12,000/480 = 15,600/x 12,000x = 480×15,600 = 7,488,000 x = 624 ✓
Takeaway: CPA is a unit rate — cost divided to get cost per one customer. Comparing unit rates across campaigns is the standard efficiency comparison in marketing analytics. The proportion setup (12000/480 = 15600/x) shows how to scale from known efficiency to new budget — classic proportional reasoning in a real analytics context.
S4Bayesian likelihood ratio — proportion in probabilityStatistics / Probability
A medical test for a disease has: sensitivity = 0.90 (P(+|disease) = 0.90) and specificity = 0.85 (P(−|no disease) = 0.85). Disease prevalence = 1%. Compute the likelihood ratio and posterior probability of disease given a positive test using Bayes' theorem.
Given: P(disease) = 0.01 P(no disease) = 0.99 P(+|disease) = 0.90 (sensitivity — true positive rate) P(+|no disease) = 0.15 (1 − specificity — false positive rate) Likelihood Ratio (LR+): LR+ = P(+|disease) / P(+|no disease) = 0.90 / 0.15 = 6.0 Interpretation: a positive test is 6× more likely if you have the disease than if you don't. This is a ratio of probabilities — pure ratio arithmetic. Bayes' Theorem (using odds form): Prior odds = P(disease)/P(no disease) = 0.01/0.99 ≈ 0.0101 Posterior odds = Prior odds × LR+ = 0.0101 × 6.0 = 0.0606 Posterior probability = 0.0606 / (1 + 0.0606) ≈ 0.0572 ≈ 5.72% Even with a positive test, probability of disease = only 5.72% (not 90%!) because the prior probability is very low (1%). This counterintuitive result — the base rate fallacy — is entirely a consequence of ratio and proportion arithmetic with probabilities.
Takeaway: The likelihood ratio is a direct ratio of two probabilities. Bayes' theorem updates beliefs by multiplying prior odds by this ratio. Understanding that a positive test doesn't mean 90% chance of disease requires ratio reasoning — the test's sensitivity is a ratio, the prevalence is a ratio, and the posterior is their proportional combination.
S5Feature scaling as proportional mappingData Preprocessing / ML
A feature column "age" has values ranging from 22 to 65. Apply min-max normalization, viewing it as a proportion problem. For an age of 40: what is the normalized value, and what proportion of the range does it represent? Then verify that standardization (z-score) can also be viewed as a ratio.
Min-max normalization: x' = (x − min) / (max − min) For x = 40: x' = (40 − 22) / (65 − 22) = 18/43 ≈ 0.4186 Proportion interpretation: "40 is what proportion of the way from 22 to 65?" Distance from min: 40 − 22 = 18 Total range: 65 − 22 = 43 Proportion = 18/43 ≈ 41.9% of the way from min to max. This IS a proportion: (x−min)/(max−min) = x'/(1−0) = x'/1 i.e., the normalized value is proportional to its position in the range. Z-score standardization as a ratio: z = (x − μ) / σ z measures: "how many standard deviations is x from the mean?" This is a ratio of two differences — measuring x's deviation as a proportion of the typical (standard) deviation. If x=50, μ=40, σ=8: z = (50−40)/8 = 10/8 = 1.25 Meaning: x is 1.25 standard deviations above the mean. = a ratio of "how far from mean" to "typical distance from mean"
Takeaway: Both normalization methods are ratio/proportion operations. Min-max normalization expresses x as a proportion of the range. Z-score expresses x's deviation as a ratio to the standard deviation. Viewing preprocessing through the lens of ratios makes the formulas interpretable rather than mechanical.
